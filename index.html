How to Play

The pieces

Each player has 12 goblets — cup-shaped pieces in four sizes: Small, Medium, Large, and XL. They start nested inside each other in three stacks beside the board, so only the outermost (biggest) goblet of each stack can be picked up. That means you always begin the game placing XLs.

The goal

Line up four goblets of your color in a row — across, down, or diagonally — on the 4×4 board. Only visible goblets count.

On your turn

Do exactly one of these:

  • Place a goblet from one of your reserve stacks onto an empty square.
  • Move one of your goblets already on the board to any other square — empty, or occupied by a strictly smaller goblet.

Gobbling

Moving onto a smaller goblet covers it — it gets “gobbled.” You can gobble your opponent’s goblets or your own. Gobbled goblets aren’t captured: they stay on the board, hidden underneath, and reappear when the goblet covering them moves away.

The rule of three

Normally, goblets from your reserve can only be placed on empty squares. Exception: if your opponent has three visible goblets in one line (the three don’t need to be adjacent — just in the same row, column, or diagonal), you may place a reserve goblet directly on top of one of those three, if yours is bigger. It’s the escape hatch against an imminent four-in-a-row — and sometimes a winning attack.

The reveal rule ⚠️

Moving a goblet uncovers whatever it was hiding. If that reveals an opponent goblet that completes their four-in-a-row — and your move doesn’t re-cover that line — you lose immediately, even if your move also completed your own line. Remember what you’ve gobbled!

Tips & tricks

  • Memory is the game. Track what’s buried where — especially which of your opponent’s big goblets are hiding under yours.
  • Don’t gobble your own pieces without reason — it ties up two of your goblets in one square.
  • Three-in-a-row cuts both ways. It threatens a win, but the rule of three makes those goblets attackable — small pieces sitting in your threat line can be gobbled straight from the opponent’s reserve.
  • Small goblets are bait. A small piece placed on a key square invites a gobble; a saved XL can take that square right back.
  • Keep an XL in reserve for late-game defense — it can land on almost anything.
  • Build double threats. Two lines that need one more goblet each can’t both be blocked.
  • Think before you lift. Moving a goblet that’s covering an opponent piece can hand them the game via the reveal rule.
